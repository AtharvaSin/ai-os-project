The World of Bharatvarsh
A narrative overview of the novel’s setting, history, society, and conflicts

A. Origins of an Alternate India

Bharatvarsh is not simply an India that modern readers recognise. Its timeline diverged when the sub-continent resisted full-scale European conquest; colonial powers such as Britain, Portugal and Spain were confined to minor coastal enclaves that yielded diminishing returns, allowing indigenous dynasties to industrialise on their own terms. By the First World War, a loosely federated Bharatvarsh had become an indispensable Allied partner, even collaborating with the United States on the atom bomb and gaining enormous diplomatic capital for ending the conflict.

Democracy flowered in the early twentieth century, yet sectarian violence and political gridlock created a vacuum. In 1978 a supposedly “one-year emergency” handed full control to the army. Four decades later—2025 AD—the country is still ruled by the General Directorate, and the first synchronised bombings by a resurgent Tribhuj movement mark the opening crisis of the novel .

B. Constitutional Reality: A Military Technocracy

The Directorate governs through three continental commands—Northern Plains, Peninsular, and the Eastern-Himalayan Treaty Zone. Parliament survives as pageantry; real authority flows from General Pratap and his circle of brigadiers. Civil courts deal with petty matters, but anything touching “national harmony” goes before martial benches. In return for obedience the regime delivers gleaming highways, net-zero towers and a crime rate that has “almost vanished” .

Religion, caste surnames and most independent media are banned. Former mosques and temples now house cancer institutes and senate chambers , architectural reminders that faith has been repurposed for state utility. Hindi is the broadcast lingua-franca; English reigns in science; scores of regional tongues persist in private.

C. Bharatvarsh and the Wider World

Internationally, Bharatvarsh carries a peace-through-strength brand. It holds a permanent UN veto, trades green technology with the EU and Japan, co-develops AI with the United States, and maintains a wary friendship with China. The absence of Pakistan and Bangladesh spares it a hostile border, yet the Eastern tribes enjoy de-facto autonomy under the Teesta–Nepal treaties; regular troops need tribal consent to enter those hills . Rival powers watch for any treaty violation to drag the army before UN courts .

D. Engines of Wealth and Innovation

Agriculture, heavy industry and defence R & D mesh into a single national economy. Solar-powered desert laboratories perfect cybernetic prosthetics and anti-ageing serums, first for soldiers, later for civilians . All discoveries spend a compulsory “defence-first” cycle in classified labs before partial release to commercial firms.

On city streets the spill-over is visible: self-driving hover-cars glide a few metres above the asphalt while passengers pay by fingerprint scanners embedded in the seat backs . Oxy-poles—tree-shaped catalytic towers—scrub exhaust, and whole façades are given over to vertical gardens that no one inhabits .

E. The Surveillance State

Safety is bought with total transparency. A nationwide lattice of cameras cross-checks faces, gaits and even emotional micro-expressions; analyst Tara can trace a suspect across multiple cities in minutes using gait alone . Digital wallets record every sale; social media sentiment is mined in real time .

Where soft power fails, hard power stands ready: shielded personnel carriers, EMP grenades, drone artillery and hypersonic helicopters whose autopilots let officers read dossiers while “not flying the chopper” .

F. Everyday Texture—Utopia with Edges

Morning in Lakshmanpur. Commuters coast above six-lane boulevards flanked by holographic billboards proclaiming “Working Hard, So you don’t have to” . Festival Fortnight— the single state-sponsored holiday—has just ended, but neon drones still patrol for curfew violators. In cafés, civilians joke that paying cash gets you stared at “as if money were a museum piece” .

Yet beneath the polish lies quiet resentment. Bus passengers complain that “army men reserve all the special perks for themselves” , and banned art circulates as graffiti or contraband VR chips. In the Andaman Cellular Jail—a vast pentagonal fortress on its own island—dissidents disappear into D-Wing’s interrogation suites, “where no one who goes in ever comes out” .

G. The Tribhuj and the Coming Storm

Long before the dictatorship, the Tribhuj began as an ascetic martial wing that protected multi-faith pilgrims during lawless decades . After the army’s rise they championed democracy, were crushed, and vanished into legend—until seven coordinated bombings in 2025 killed hundreds and tarnished Bharatvarsh’s aura of invulnerability .

While official broadcasts brand the group a terrorist cult “declared treasonous for almost half a century” , field officers note troubling inconsistencies: the historical Tribhuj vowed never to harm innocents . As rumours spread, mountain tribes, urban intellectuals and even disillusioned officers weigh whether the attacks are liberationist vengeance or a black-flag operation engineered inside the army itself—especially after whispers of a coming solar-flare blackout surface in the General’s own war room .

H. Themes in Tension

Security versus Freedom – Citizens enjoy clean air and free healthcare, but surrender privacy and dissent.

Meritocracy versus Privilege – In theory all ranks are open; in practice the uniform is a social passport .

Progress versus Soul-loss – Vertical gardens and anti-ageing serum cannot replace shuttered temples or banned poetry.

Control versus Chaos – The army’s obsession with order ironically breeds the very insurgency it fears.

I. Conclusion

Bharatvarsh stands as a shimmering super-state: carbon-negative, crime-free, and richer than any version of India that history has yet produced. But its perfection is brittle. The resurrected Tribhuj, the simmering tribe-state tensions of the Himalaya, and rival generals plotting coups behind biometric vaults all hint that harmony obtained by force is harmony forever at risk of fracture. This is the stage on which the novel’s protagonists must decide whether to defend the elegant cage, dismantle it, or build something unknown beyond its gilded bars.

C2) Band-by-Band environment behavior

Three altitude bands govern color/contrast, moisture, and edge wear.

Foothill Green (≈ 900–1,600 m)

Cold Spruce (≈ 1,600–2,800 m)

Surface behavior. Wind scoring on slate edges; rope fibers chalk with frost; bronze handles warm up with touch.

Typical vignettes. Switchbacks; caltrops flagged with marigold dots; silent lanes on ridges.

High Alpine (≈ 2,800–4,200 m)

Palette feel. Blue-grey rocks; sparse greens; snow/ice dominate micro-spec.

Surface behavior. Crusted snow (not powder) holds boot edges; matte ice sits like glass with micro-pitting.

Typical vignettes. Pikas skitter; breath plumes; flags stiff, not fluttery; sound becomes small.

No-fly valleys are absolute. No aircraft, no hovercams, no drones inside those cones.

Quiet tech. Radios and Brace-Comm run on silent bands; mesh nodes stay outside “signal-quiet rings” at Council and School.

Signage & color. Tribal header + English subhead; caution Marigold on Deep Green; hazard Vermilion on Slate.

Material truth. Traps and defenses look hand-made (timber, rope, stone, felt, bronze/iron), not sci-fi.

E1) Movement Grammar (how people, herds, and convoys actually move)

E1.1 Trail classes (visual + rules)

Ridge Silent Lanes (windward spines): narrow, staked with marigold dot-stakes; used by runners and sentries.

Visual: compacted snow line, felted boot polish at edges; low cairns every 70–100 paces.

Rule: whisper-only; no bells, no tinware; handsign code active.

Terrace Work Paths (between huts, pens, granaries): broadest; sled ruts in winter; duckboards in monsoon.

Visual: rake marks; rope lash points on corners; “Yield to Herds” pictos.

Valley Corridors (approach roads, rope-bridges): where outsiders arrive.

Visual: treaty gate boards; Treaty Glyph plaques; rope-bridge silhouettes and avalanche boards.

E1.2 Step & footing doctrine

Rock-tongue first: step onto natural stone tongues; avoid glitter sheens.

Probe, then place: staff taps twice at ankle depth before weight; kids use color-coded sticks (red = adult only).

Counter count: 60 paces per trail module; tally cut into posts; resets at each cairn.

Whiteout line: hemp guide ropes along exposed spans; knots every 5 paces; cloth flags every 20.

E1.3 Switchback protocols (for climbs/descents)

Eyes-up corners: convex mirrors are replaced by shale reflectors (slate plates) set at 45°.

Turn-call: leader taps staff twice at each hairpin; rear echoes once.

Landslide clock: after 15:00 (pre-monsoon), descent only; uphill movement restricted to local writ.

E1.4 Rope-bridge & river shelf crossing

One-third rule: never more than one third the planks occupied; runners cross last.

Lash check: visual on four anchor knots and midspan joint before crossing; lashing pictos show “OK/NOT OK”.

Wind gate: above Beaufort ~6 equivalent (palms bend, flags snap straight), crossing halts.

E1.5 Night & blackout movement

New-moon convoy: no lamps; cat-eye beads on collars; staff-tip glow caps (hooded) allowed.

Sound discipline: felt wraps on metal; no clack from staff ferrules; wool mufflers on harness rings.

Checkpoint chant: soft tribal call/response (two-syllable code, changes weekly).

E1.6 Child routes & school circuits

Yellow-thread routes: staked child trails with Marigold dots every 8–10 paces; adult shade points every 100–120 paces.

Muster grid (school yard): lettered rows A–D (Tribal), numerals in EN below; arrows to Raven Span fallback.

Chaperone cadence: eldest front, teacher rear; dogs flank; pace count half-speed in fog.

E1.7 Herd movement windows

Dawn upslope / Dusk downslope spring & autumn; mid-morning only in winter (to avoid black ice); avoid ridge silent lanes during parley/ceremony hours.

E2) Defensive Architecture & Devices (non-lethal → lethal escalation)

E2.1 Perimeter zoning (hamlets, Council, School)

Observation Ring (~60–90 m out): pine nests with bow rests; mirrored slate reflectors.

Buffer Ring (~30–60 m): silent alarm triplines; bolas cache posts; smoke pots (white only, windward).

Inner Ring (~0–30 m): rope-bar gates; caltrop mats (lifted for market hours); shield boards stored at thresholds.

Rule of display: Non-lethal tools visible and reachable; lethal tools stored inside armories with writ postings—never on public walls.

E2.2 Silent alarms (no bells near Quiet Rings)

Felt-head thumpers: weighted pucks on triplines that thud against plank shields.

Sand flutes: slow hiss in hollow reed at gate posts (monsoon-safe).

Prayer-wheel “stallers”: weighted to spin once and halt—visual cue for sentries without sound.

E2.3 Non-lethal devices (default response)

Bolas posts: two-cord, leather weights; marked Caution yellow bands.

Caltrop mats: fold-out, slat-based; hazard board posted 10 paces ahead; stored rolled.

Sap-dart throwers: low-draw ballistas (10–15 m effective) using sleep saps; red cap kept on triggers in peace hours.

Net drops: weighted mesh from porch beams; release pegs above thresholds.

E2.4 Lethal escalation (invaders only; never near School/Dojo/Sanctuaries)

Rockslide levers: painted Vermilion/Slate; sited outside residential arcs; clear “FALL LINE” boards.

Deadfall gates: spiked beams (timber/iron) at choke alleys; warning boards in Tribal only (inner-side).

Arrow slits: in stone corners; dust curtains hide openings when not in use.

Spearnests: at pine platforms; backup javelins; writ stored at nest ladder (invader-only clause).

E2.5 Drone & air denial

Jammer totems: carved posts with copper coils; Treaty No-Fly boards at valley mouths; placed outside School/Council quiet rings.

Reflective veils: fabric nets across narrow cuts; visually read as prayer-cloth but functionally occlude UAV optics.

Heat discipline: stoves baffled; vents rerouted under stone in watch hours.

E3) Patrols, Posts & Protocols

E3.1 Sentry shifts & patterns

Two-up rule: never alone; pairs offset by 5–7 paces.

Shift ladder: Dawn (grey) → Midday → Dusk → Midnight (short); rotate nests clockwise.

Sightlines: every nest must see at least two others; mirror signals on cloud days.

E3.2 Challenge & writ

Token bark: two-syllable Tribal challenge; response changes weekly.

Writ cords: braided pass tokens with knot code; cut and re-tie each lunar cycle.

Outsider compliance: stand on Caution stripe; hands visible; weapons peace-tied.

E3.3 Mesh & quiet rings

Mesh nodes: wooden mast 4.2 m; panel faces ridge; icon on pylon outside ring.

Quiet ring: 30 m radius around Council/School; boards: QUIET BAND — COMMS OFF; spot checks polite, swift.

E5) Child-Safety & Sanctuary Enforcement

No-Kill Zones: School yard, Dojo spectator rail, Learning-house porches, Monastery shelves. Boards at every entry; hazard devices disabled within 30 m.

Muster discipline: roll call at grid; teacher carries Tribal+EN card set; secondary muster at Raven Span trail if square unsafe.

Predator protocol: dogs form wedge; children behind shield boards; no arrows unless all children behind solid cover.

E6) Emergency Response & Medical

Avalanche: shout down-slope only; pull rope-gate across exposed lanes; search by probe line; color flag for “found” vs. “clear”.

Landslide: halt movement after 15:00 (pre-monsoon); detour boards; lever lines locked.

Fire: bucket chains from cisterns; stove caps on; ember rugs over thatch eaves.

Medical: triage at Clinic Nook (School) or infirmary bench (Council); warm packs; boiled tools; sled evac to helipad at Juniper Gate (permissioned only, outside no-fly).

Comms: mesh ping to neighboring hamlets; Brace-Comm on silent band; quiet rings remain enforced.

E7) Drone-Avoidance & Optics Denial

Overhead occlusion: timber pergolas over key lanes; cloth veils in storm season; canopies break LOS from ridge cams.

Heat masking: stove baffles; vent through ground runs; thermal signature diluted.

Glare trick: set shale reflectors to flash sky color at common UAV viewpoints—confuses auto-exposure, not a weapon.

Phase F — Tech & Utility Integration (Lock v1)

F0) Design pillars (what every asset should express)

Quiet, alt-advanced tech. Solutions are low-glare, hand-serviceable, and storm-proof. Wood/stone/slate first; metals are darkened, oiled, or oxidized. No neon, no chrome.

Autonomy over spectacle. Gear integrates into terraces and roofs; utility is backgrounded behind ritual and daily life.

Safety signage. Tribal Script header + English subhead; Marigold on Deep Green for caution; Vermilion on Slate for hazards.

Doctrines in force. Non-lethal first (tribe↔tribe and civilian spaces); invader-only lethal escalations; no-fly valleys absolute.

F1) Power & Heat

F1.1 Micro-Hydro Wheel (rill-drive)

Purpose. Base electricity for lights, radios, and clinics without inviting attention or noise.

Site fit. Mounted on a stone cradle beside a stepped rill; intake at a small forebay with a leaf-screen.

Envelope. Wheel Ø 1.6 m, width 0.45 m; cedar paddles on blackened-iron spokes; bronze axle bushings.

Head/flow. Works from 1.2–2.5 m head; optimal ~1.8 m with 50–80 L/s flow.

Shaft & drive. Leather-belt to a slow generator in a timber hut (600×900 mm footprint), felt-lined for sound damping.

Covers. Split-plank cowling over top quadrant; winter ice-lip deflector; child-safe rail at 900 mm height.

Signage. Pylon:
Tribal: “Rill Wheel — Keep Clear”
EN: MICRO-HYDRO — KEEP CLEAR (Caution colorway)

Visual tells. Wet sheen on paddles; moss at splash zone; muted thrum. No bright wiring; hemp-wrapped leads where exposed.

F1.2 Roof-Slate Solar (stealth PV)

Purpose. Daytime trickle charge for lamps, Brace-Comm, clinic tools.

Panels. Dark blue-grey “slate” laminates, 300×450 mm, built into the roofing grid; matte anti-glare finish.

Array size. 12–36 tiles per large roof; 4–12 tiles per cabin.

Wiring. Under-slate channels to a clay-lined junction box; braided cloth-sheathed cable drops to interior.

Winter/monsoon. Snow hooks keep sliding sheets; drip chains route water away from junctions.

Visual tells. Panels disappear until they catch a soft, cold specular line; never the shiny glassy look.

F1.3 Heat & Stoves (iron + stone)

Purpose. Clean heat, cooking, and safe venting without smoke signatures at sensitive hours.

Stove body. Blackened iron, 600×400×500 mm; door with mica window; rope-wrapped pull.

Flue. 100–120 mm pipe into a stone heat-sink wall; hidden baffles for thermal draw; spark arrestor cap at roof (mesh cone).

Fuel. Split pine; yak-dung cakes (outer rings only); ash bucket with lid.

Quiet mode. Bypass through under-stone channels before exiting at a low wall, diffusing thermal plume.

Visual tells. Warm rim highlights at 2700–3000 K; soot bloom where flue meets stone; felt draught snakes at door bottoms.

F2) Water, Drainage & Sanitation

F2.1 Melt-Cisterns (non-sacred)

Form. Stone-lined, lid-covered cisterns; Ø 1.2–1.8 m; depth 0.8–1.2 m; rope-handle lid.

Inflow. Roof chain bowls → runnels → settling pan → cistern; screen basket catches debris.

Signage.
Tribal: “Covered Water”
EN: CISTERN — KEEP LID ON (Caution)

Visual tells. Ice bloom at rim in winter; mineral rings on stone; drip grooves at bowl edges.

F2.2 Drainage grammar (monsoon logic)

Runnels. Shallow V-grooves cut along alleys; fall to gravel pans; algae tint in storms.

Duckboards. Plank walkways across yard low points; lash points for gusts.

Overflows. Carved lion-mouth spouts simplified to clean ellipses—water shoots clear of walls.

F2.3 Latrines & Compost (leeward, dry)

Placement. Down-slope, leeward of dwellings; 15–25 m off lanes; never in school/dojo rings.

Pit & box. Timber box with lid; ash bucket; lime scoop; hand-wash pitcher and drain trench.

Signage. Small Tribal + EN icon board; no oversized text.

Visual tells. Matte timber, no plastic seats; a discreet bucket chain; marigold dot on the path stake.

F3) Communications & Signal Discipline

F3.1 Mesh Node Mast (LoS, outside Quiet Rings)

Mast. 4.2 m timber post; triple-guyed; small LoS panel (350×300 mm) facing ridge.

Base. Stone pad; lash cleats; lightning strap to buried rod.

Power. Slate-PV trickle + hydro buffer; battery in wood box (vented).

Icon. Mesh pictogram (three chevrons feeding a dot) at cap-height on a Deep-Green pylon.

Rule. Masts must sit outside 30 m signal-quiet rings around Council & School. Inside the ring: QUIET BAND — COMMS OFF boards.

F3.2 Brace-Comm etiquette (human behavior)

Bands. Silent band default; burst packets on the hour only in curfew nights; village code rolls weekly.

Posture. Brace at chest, eyes up; never mid-conversation; antennae shrouded in felt.

Scrub. After parley or alarm drills, logs wiped at Council clerk’s board.

F3.3 Jammers & No-Fly Markers (treaty enforcement)

Jammer totem. Carved post with copper coil helix; buried box; grounded to slate plate. Don’t over-design—should read as local craft + copper glint.

Boards at mouths.
Tribal: “No Sky — Treaty Sanctuary”
EN: NO-FLY VALLEY — TREATY SANCTUARY (Hazard colorway, Vermilion on Slate)

Optics denial. Reflective shale plates to kick skylight toward common UAV view angles (exposure spoof, not a weapon).

F4) Medical — Clinics, Kits & Evac

F4.1 Clinic Nook (School) & Infirmary Bench (Council)

School Clinic Nook (6×3 m).

Hygiene. Boiling pot; drying rack; sealed bin; soap & ash.

F4.2 Med Kits (layered)

Pocket kit. Cloth roll: bandage, sling, splinter forceps, salve, tourniquet strap, sugar/salt sachets.

Patrol kit. Sled bag: blankets, hot-water bladders, splints (shin/forearm), ophthalmo shield, sap antidote.

Clinic chest. Boiled tools, sutures, gauze, tinctures, bone needle, lantern with spare wick, pulse oximeter (low-power), spare Brace battery.

Visual tells. Cloth wraps, corked bottles, matte metal instruments; no plastic boxes.

F4.3 Evac & Helipad (permissioned only)

Primary evac. Sled to Juniper Gate outer terrace; stretcher hand-off; helipad ring in painted Marigold on Stone.

Helipad hardware. Stone ring Ø 12 m, low cairns at cardinal points, wind sock on timber pole, Treaty Gate board nearby (Tribal + EN).

No-fly doctrine. Pad sits outside no-fly valleys; flights by writ only; hover berth never over valleys.

9) Director’s Intent (one page to onboard anyone)

Tone. Resilient, humane, austere. The Wilds are not “primitive”—they’re deliberately quiet: wood, stone, slate, felt, oxidized metal, alt-advanced craft. Technology serves people and terrain rather than announcing itself.

Ethos. Non-lethal first isn’t softness—it’s discipline. Lethal force is for invaders only. Schools and dojos are No-Kill sanctuaries. Dojo identity is the Grey Trident on Orange—never replaced by treaty branding.

Look. Kaali’s greens lead the identity; Marigold cautions; Vermilion on Slate warns. Carving, stitching, and patina replace neon and chrome. Winter whiteouts reduce the world to breath and shape; monsoon sculpts stone with water; pre-monsoon glare demands matte, not gloss.

Movement. Trails are logic: ridge Silent Lanes, terrace Work Paths, valley Corridors with treaty gates. Count paces, probe steps, read stakes. Children follow Yellow-thread routes; dogs flank; bell ropes speak quietly.

What never happens. Drones in no-fly pockets. Chrome and neon. Tricolor as décor. Dojo flags co-branded. Loud tech where silence is law.

A) Topo & Climate Cards

Three distinct geographies drive how light, air, water, and crowds behave. Each card gives: seasonal cycles, sky/air behavior, material responses, flora silhouette rules, and weather vignettes to reuse in environments.

A1. Lakshmanpur — Northern River-Plain Metropole

Geographic read. Broad alluvial plain; a meandering river with levees and shallow flood terraces; low relief; dense inner city giving way to regimented new sectors.

Seasonal cycle (visual consequences).

Winter (Dec–Jan): Cool and fog-prone mornings. Low contrast; soft shadows. OxyPoles ghost in haze.
Lighting recipe: 5800–6200K diffuse key; low specular; aerial perspective on mid-rises.

Pre-Monsoon (Apr–May): Hot, dusty “brass heat”; glare off pale stone/concrete; heat shimmer above hover lanes.
Recipe: 5200–5600K hard key; high contrast; desat mid-tones; faint amber in highlights.

Monsoon (Jun–Sep): Heavy rain; mirror asphalt; saturated greens on vertical gardens; river swells.
Recipe: 6200–6800K overcast day / 3500–4200K mixed urban night; strong reflections; puddle caustics.

Post-Monsoon (Oct–Nov): Clearer skies; fresh vegetation; evening air crisp.

Materials & surfaces. Pale limestone/sandstone; pale concrete; oxidized bronze rails; ceramic jali panels. Fog reduces apparent saturation—lean into texture (micro-pitting, efflorescence) over color.

Flora silhouettes. Tall columnar street trees for sightline continuity; planter trenches at medians; climber-faced façades on east/north to catch soft light.

Weather vignettes (reusable).

“Milk-glass morning”: human silhouettes emerge through fog; muted banners; OxyPole halos.

“Brass noon”: wavering road markings, mirage ripples at 20–30 m.

“Monsoon mirror night”: puddled neon; hovercam cones reflected; raindrop bokeh.

A2. Mysuru — Interior Plateau Tech-Hub

Geographic read. Slight elevation; granitic shelves; dry winters and a pronounced wet season. Crisp horizons; stronger sun angles.

Seasonal cycle (visual consequences).

Dry Season (Dec–Mar): Clear air, hard shadows; stone warms quickly.
Recipe: 5400–6000K; high contrast; strong occlusion shadows; speculars on polished basalt.

Pre-Monsoon (Apr–Jun): Heat build-up; dust in the late afternoons; vegetation slightly stressed.

Monsoon (Jul–Sep): Sudden lushness; heavy sheets of rain; runoff rills off terraces.
Recipe: 6000–6600K overcast; glossy stone; directional rain streaks; waterfall gutters.

Shoulder (Oct–Nov): Pleasant; balanced light; good time for civic ceremonies.

Materials & surfaces. Granite, basalt plinths, laterite blocks; fiber-cement louvers; matte ceramic in plazas (no specular glare during dry months).

Flora silhouettes. Xeric shrubs and hardy lawn bands in dry months; pergola vines over transit forecourts; rooftop gardens with shallow-soil species.

Weather vignettes.

“Granite glare noon”: bold shadow geometry; hover lanes read as crisp ribbons.

“Monsoon terrace sheet”: rain cascading from step-eaves; water chains beating in courtyards.

A3. Kolkata Corridor — Deltaic Aerotropolis

Geographic read. Lowland delta; embankments; mangrove influence; very humid summers; short winter; frequent pre-monsoon evening squalls.

Seasonal cycle (visual consequences).

Pre-Monsoon (Mar–May): Humid, storm-prone evenings; turbulent clouds; sodium street pools + distant lightning.
Recipe: 6200–7000K storm decks by day; 3200–3800K sodium pools at night with cool spill from holo screens.

Monsoon (Jun–Sep): Long, wind-driven rain; flooded curb edges; soft, low-contrast days; thunderheads.

Winter (Dec–Jan): Brief, mild; clearer nights; cooler daylight.

Post-Monsoon (Oct–Nov): Sparkling air after downpours; algae sheens on stone near water.

Materials & surfaces. Corrosion-aware metals; anti-slip terracotta tiles; sealed concrete for salt-air; deep balconies with rain-chains.

Flora silhouettes. Wind-flex palms; mangrove-inspired planters near waterways; lush planter boxes on skywalks.

Weather vignettes.

“Nor’wester street”: banners whipping, raindrops at oblique, reflections streaked by wind.

“Blue-hour levee”: puddled embankments, long sodium reflections, hover berths pulled back from the waterline.

B) Typography & Signage Spec (Tri-Script System)

Every public sign uses a three-line hierarchy: English (Latin) → Hindi (Devanagari) → State line (Telugu for Mysuru, Bengali for Kolkata Corridor, Hindi repeats for Lakshmanpur where state line overlaps). Typeface choices emphasize legibility, wide glyph coverage, and consistent stroke logic.

Type families (lock).

Latin (English): Inter — weights 400/600/800; variable axis allowed in UI and large-format signs.

Devanagari (Hindi): Noto Sans Devanagari — 400 (body) / 600 (wayfinding) / 800 (monumental).

Telugu (Mysuru state line): Noto Sans Telugu — 400/600/800.

Bengali (Kolkata state line): Noto Sans Bengali — 400/600/800.

Stacking & layout (apply everywhere).

Header (facility/road name): EN / Inter 800 / ALL CAPS / +5% tracking / Optical size ≥ 72 pt (physical signs: 120–220 mm cap height depending on throw).

Subhead (Hindi): Noto Dev 600 / Title Case / optical size −1 tier from header.

State line: Noto Telugu/Bengali 600 / same point size as Hindi; if lines exceed width, wrap state line first.

Body copy: All three scripts at 400; use tabular numerals; metric units first.

Grid. 8-pt baseline grid for print/UI; physical signs use an 8-mm module. Margins ≥ 3 modules; line gap = 1–1.25× cap height.

Pictograms (lock set).

Biometric gate (thumb + face outline): shown as paired icon to the left of text;

QR gate (square finder corners + arrow);

Hover lane (0–5 m hover band icon);

OxyPole (stylized trunk + halo leaves);

Curfew (moon + barred stripe).
Stroke: 2.5 px (UI) / 6–10 mm (physical); rounded joins; no gradients.

Contrast & legibility.

Minimum contrast aim: ≥ 4.5:1 for all text/background combinations.

Day mode signs: dark text (#0B1D2A) on light panels (#F2F5F7).

Night mode: backlit panels with reversed text (white on #0B1D2A) and luminous accent bands.

Emblem & flag usage.

State emblem appears top-right of government signage; safe area = emblem width all around; never scaled under 24 mm.

Tricolor reserved for flags, ceremony backdrops, and “Authority” bands on official gantries; not used as gradients or textures.

2) Global Acceptance Criteria (GAC)

For a board/shot to pass:

GAC-01 – Language order. All public signage uses tri-script in this order:

Header: English (Inter 800, ALL CAPS).

Subhead: Hindi (Noto Sans Devanagari 600).

State line: Telugu (Mysuru) or Bengali (Kolkata corridor) at 600.
Body copy (if present): 400 weight in all three. Fail if order or weight deviates.

GAC-02 – Emblem & flag etiquette.

State emblem only on government furniture/pylons; safe area = emblem width on all sides; min size 24 mm physical.

Tricolor appears only on flags, ceremony backdrops, and “Authority” bands on official gantries. Never as textures, grunge, or banners.

GAC-03 – Festival overlay coverage. Annual palettes may skin banners/drones/kiosks/UI chrome only, ≤ 30% of visible area (≤ 20% inside sacred plots). Base civic palette must dominate.

GAC-04 – OxyPole spacing & states.

Spacing: 60–80 m on boulevards; 40–50 m on festival axes.

Night luminance ≤ 200 cd/m²; cyan tick ≤ 2% of crown area; no neon glow tubes.

GAC-05 – Hover band clarity.

Operational band 0–5 m. Beacons at ~25 m cadence; egress pads 6×10 m (±); amber perimeter ticks every 1 m. Fail if vehicles appear over water (delta) or beyond 5 m in non-emergency scenes.

GAC-06 – Surveillance as second-read.

Checkpoint masts: slim, unarmed silhouettes; never center-framed unless story-critical.

Drones: rim-lit/bokeh/partial—not hero subjects in civic beauty shots.

GAC-07 – Heritage perimeter rules.

No drones over roofs; algorithmic mast ≥ 25 m away; scanners only on civic side of the buffer, never at thresholds.

Heritage signage uses two-line stack (EN header + State subhead). Hindi optional as small body line.

GAC-08 – Climate honesty.

Lakshmanpur: fog in winter, heat shimmer pre-monsoon, mirror asphalt in monsoon.

Mysuru: dry-season hard shadows; monsoon terrace sheets; dust film pre-monsoon.

Kolkata corridor: humidity bloom; flood logic (flood line +250 mm), nor’wester wind/rain vectors.

GAC-09 – Contrast & accessibility.

Text/background contrast ≥ 4.5:1; tactile curb nodes present at crossings; at least one accessible gate (wider lane) in metro banks.

GAC-10 – Utility brands domain.

Ahuja (mobility), Mehta (power/lighting), Junaid (structural), Verma (optics/HUD), Shastri (polymers/fixtures). No cross-contamination (e.g., don’t put “Verma” on steel gantries).

If any single GAC fails, the shot is flagged for correction.

Heritage scanners at threshold. → Move to civic side beyond buffer.

Acoustic law: any sound above the posted dB cap is illegal (active monitoring).

2) System Behaviors & Exact Numbers (final)

A. Sound-limit enforcement

Street cap: 75 dB(A) at 1 m in curfew windows; 85 dB(A) during festival hours.

Hovercams & pole mics triangulate; AMBER soft warning at 90% of limit; RED spot-fine at breach.

Repeat within 10 minutes → mute zone (directional dampeners) over the violator’s bubble; log increments to “repeat” tier.

B. Density & dwell

Max standing density: 3.5 persons/m² (festival), 2.0 persons/m² (normal).

Dwell cap at vendor ring: 8 minutes per ID during festival; 3 minutes on ordinary days at choke points.

Exceed → AMBER nudge on wrist + kiosk arrow to move; second exceed in 30 min → RED fine + relocation prompt.

C. Lane logic (per side)

Lane A (Ped): 2.4–3.0 m wide, textured pavers, embedded light-strips with GREEN/AMBER/RED ticks every 5 m.

Lane B (Auto-flow): glide-car stream; no stop except flagged pull-outs.

Lane C (Auto-service): kerbside service + authorized stops; quick-scan bollards at pull-offs.

D. Hovercam status (LED halo + “eye”)

Green: passive patrol; audio off; LED eyes dim.

Amber: observation focus; local PA primed; slow orbit at 6–8 m AGL.

Red: enforcement; spotlight cone tightens; video to chain-of-custody; PA live.

E. Checkpoint ladder (per individual)

First breach: verbal warning (PA), on-wrist nudge, no fine.

Second (7-day window): ₹250 spot-fine; ID flagged “observed.”

Third: ₹1000 fine + 2-hr movement restriction on the grid (soft-gates redirect).

Fourth: detain to curb-pen in Lane C; supervisor review.

F. Candy-bot protocol (minors)

Safe look: rounded cube, LED eyes (soft white/blue), mouth slot for token.

Flow: “Scan → small task → token.” Tasks = step onto pad, hold still, follow line for 3 seconds.

Reward: Candy tokens redeemable at marked kiosks (sugar or sugar-free).

Optics: always supervised by overhead hovercam (eye and bot share the same color state).

Hard rule: no identity enrollment beyond thumb; guardians can opt-out at kiosks.

G. Dispersal

Tooling: air-curtain pulses (non-injurious), fine water mist, dye-mist (expirable, 48 hrs), directional sound sweeps max 88 dB(A).

Trigger: RED density + rising dB + ignition signature (fireworks) or thrown-object vector.

H. Exemptions (visual tags)

MED-EX: medical transit (white chevron on powder-blue badge).

UTIL-EX: utilities/repairs (amber wrench glyph).

PRESS-EMB: accredited press embed (navy plate + qr-glyph).

DIGN-ESC: escorted dignitary (state seal strip + time window).

I. Transparency board (public veneer)

District wall displays: last 30 days incidents aggregate (heatmap + counts).

Small print: “For individual record access, scan here.” (Link gates to redacted public view.)

Back-end reality: full fidelity retained in private vaults (not advertised).
